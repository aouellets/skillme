# Formatted Report Writer

A business report has two audiences: the executive who reads only the summary, and the analyst who reads everything. A good report serves both without making either feel like a second-class reader.

## Before writing: purpose and audience

Answer in one sentence each:
- What decision or action does this report enable?
- Who is the primary reader, and what do they already know?
- What does the reader need to believe by the end?

If these are unclear, the report structure will be unclear. Do not begin drafting until these are settled.

## Document skeleton

Default structure for a business or consulting report:
1. Title block — title, author, date, version
2. Executive summary — 150 to 250 words; stands alone; includes the recommendation
3. Background / context — shared facts and scope boundaries
4. Findings — the substance, organized by theme not chronology
5. Analysis — what the findings mean and why it matters
6. Recommendations — numbered, prioritized, actionable
7. Appendix — supporting data, methodology, raw research

Each section begins with a one-sentence statement of what it covers. Readers navigating quickly will thank you.

## Heading hierarchy

Three levels maximum. Use them consistently:
- H1 for major sections (numbered in formal reports)
- H2 for subsections within a major section
- H3 only when a subsection has meaningful internal structure

Never use heading levels for visual emphasis. If content needs emphasis, bold a sentence or use a callout box.

## Skimmability rules

- No paragraph longer than 6 lines in the findings and analysis sections
- Every section has a clear topic sentence in the first or second line
- Use numbered lists for sequences and ranked items; bullet lists for parallel non-sequential items
- Tables beat prose for any comparison of 3 or more items across 2 or more attributes
- Bold the key term or finding once per paragraph — not every important word

## Tone and register

Match the register to the context: formal for board and regulatory documents, plain for internal ops reports. Default to plain — clear sentences over impressive vocabulary. A report that requires a second read to be understood has failed.

Passive voice is a red flag. 'Revenue declined 18%' beats 'An 18% decline in revenue was observed.'
