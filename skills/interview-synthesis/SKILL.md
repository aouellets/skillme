# Interview Synthesis

Synthesis is where data becomes insight. The job is not to summarize what people said — it is to identify the underlying pattern that explains why they said it and what it means for design or strategy.

## Prepare Your Data

Before synthesizing, ensure every interview has a cleaned transcript or structured notes with participant ID, date, and key verbatim quotes marked. Do not rely on memory. Minimum viable dataset: 5 interviews for foundational themes; fewer requires explicitly noting low confidence.

## Extract Observations First, Interpretations Second

Pass one: extract atomic observations — one idea per sticky note or row. These are factual ('Participant said she checks the app every morning before coffee') not interpretive. Pass two: group observations by similarity, ignoring the source participant. Name each cluster with a verb-noun label ('avoids manual entry', 'relies on notifications as reminders'). Only then elevate a cluster to an insight.

## Writing Insights

An insight is a non-obvious, actionable statement that connects observed behavior to an underlying motivation or friction. Format: '[User type] [does behavior] because [motivation/belief], but [tension or barrier].' Weak: 'Users find notifications helpful.' Strong: 'Power users treat push notifications as their primary task queue because they distrust the in-app home screen, but this breaks down when notification volume exceeds ~15 per day.'

## Selecting Supporting Quotes

Each insight needs one strong verbatim quote and ideally two corroborating ones from different participants. A strong quote is specific, vivid, and free of researcher interpretation. Anonymize to 'P3' or persona label before sharing externally.

## Confidence and Frequency

Tag each insight with frequency (how many participants) and confidence (high / medium / low). A single-participant observation is a signal, not a finding — keep it in an 'outliers worth watching' section rather than promoting it to a theme.

## Output Structure

Deliver findings as: executive summary (3-5 bullets), themes table (theme name, insight statement, frequency, confidence, top quote), and a 'so what' recommendation for each theme. Outliers and open questions go in an appendix.
